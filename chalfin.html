Chalfin

In a flat stretch of prairie near the base of the bluff where the present day Maeystown Creek (known to early settlers as Worley’s creek) enters the flatland of the bottom, sits the once small community of Chalfin Bridge. Named after the bridge that crossed the Creek, the area was settled in 1796 by Isaac and William Chalfin, and at least 5 generations of Chalfins are buried in the cemetery in the old graveyard near the bluff. Though modern-day Bluff Road has been rerouted to a new bridge a few hundred yards away, the former alignment of the road still points to an abandoned and derelict bridge that barely spans the eroding banks of the Creek. // This stretch of the Monroe Bottoms is part of the Mitchie township—a designation given to this precinct by the Mitchegamie Indians who inhabited this portion of the floodplain. The Mitchegamie were a tribe of the Illinois Confederacy, with a trio of Illini settlements in the Bottoms numbered nearly as many Native Americans as French in the mid 18th century. Between Chalfin Bridge and the river is an area called by the French Prairie Apocquois, for another group of Native Americans who lived there at the time.
